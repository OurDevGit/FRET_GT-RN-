Glossary Of Musical Terms
Here you will find a list of music terms along with explanations and definitions.
Accidental A note that is found in the space between two letters of the Musical Alphabet that are a whole step apart. Because there are five whole steps in the Musical Alphabet, there are five Accidentals between those whole steps. An Accidental can be named as a sharp or flat note. The five Accidentals named as sharp notes are: A#, C#, D#, F#, G#. The five Accidentals named as flat notes are: Bb, Db, Eb, Gb, Ab.
Arpeggio A chord that is played one note at a time. An Ascending Arpeggio is played from the lowest notes of the chord to the highest notes of the chord. A Descending Arpeggio is played from the highest notes of the chord to the lowest notes of the chord. A Sequenced Arpeggio plays the notes of the chord out of order. Any chord on the guitar can be played as an Arpeggio, and any Arpeggio can be played with many different rhythmic variations.
Bar A series of two or more beats that have been grouped together, also called a measure of music.
Bar Line A vertical line used to separate measures of music.
Barre A guitar technique that uses a single finger to hold down notes on two or more strings at the same time. Variations of this technique include the Partial Barre where a single finger is used to hold down notes on a few of the strings of the guitar, and the Full Barre where a single finger is used to hold down notes on all six strings of the guitar.
Bass Note Strums A guitar technique that breaks up a chord into two parts: The root note or Bass Note of the chord, which is picked as an individual note, and the remaining notes of the chord, which are strummed. The Bass Note Strum technique can be applied to any chord on the guitar, and can be played with many different rhythmic variations.
Beat A steady, even pulse that is the most fundamental element of music. Music can have a fast or slow Beat.
Bend One of the most difficult but important guitar techniques to master. A single note is picked on one string and the fingers of the left hand are then used to bend the string up to a higher pitch. This is often done using two or three fingers at the same time, which is called a reinforced bend. This technique requires a lot of physical strength, and close attention must be paid to the accuracy of the pitch that is being bent into. The most common bends use a half step or whole step interval.
Bend And Release A variation of the bend technique. A single note is picked on one string and the fingers of the left hand are then used to bend the string up to a higher pitch. Once the higher pitch has been reached, the note that has been bent is then released back to its original pitch. The bend and release technique is used to create the sound of a Melody with three parts: the pitch of the original note, then the higher pitch of the bent note, and then the pitch of the original note again. Each pitch within this three part Melody will also have its own rhythmic value, so close attention must be paid to the left hand bending technique as well as the rhythmic sound of the bend and release.
Bend And Vibrato

A variation of the bend technique. A single note is picked on one string and the fingers of the left hand are then used to bend the string up to a higher pitch. Once the fingers have bent the string into position and the higher pitch has been reached, the vibrato technique is then added to the higher pitched note. This is one of the most challenging technique to master, and requires a lot of physical strength. The left hand fingers must be able to bend the string to the correct pitch while also "shaking" the string to create the sound of vibrato. This technique takes the most practice to develop.
Box Pattern A scale form that covers a range of four or five frets, also known as a "Box Scale" or "Position Scale". This is because the scale form is played in a specific position on the neck, and the shape of the scale form can be viewed as a box of notes which contains the scale tones. Learning to play scales on the guitar using box patterns is very common. Once the individual positions or boxes are learned, then many forms can be combined together to cover the entire range of the instrument using the notes of a single Key or scale.
Chord A group of three or more notes that are played at the same time.
Chord Formula The specific notes of a scale that are used to build a chord. When you encounter a Chord Formula, it will be written using numbered Scale Tones, as in "1st" "3rd" "5th". A good understanding of Scale Tones will help you to better understand and interpret Chord Formulas.
Chord Progression A series of two or more chords that are played in a row. A Chord Progression must have at least two chords, but there is no limit to how many chords can be used.
Chord Tones The individual notes that are used to build a chord. Every chord in music contains specific notes from the Chromatic Scale. The specific notes that are used to build a chord are what determine the chord type.
Chord Voicing The specific order, amount, or arrangement of Chord Tones on the neck of the guitar. In music, one chord can have many different Voicings on the neck of the guitar. Once the individual notes of a chord have been defined, those Chord Tones can be arranged in various combinations all over the guitar fretboard.
Chromatic Scale A twelve note scale that contains all seven letters of the Musical Alphabet along with the five accidentals. Each note in the Chromatic Scale is separated from the next by the interval of a half step. The twelve notes in the Chromatic Scale are the basis for all contemporary music. 
Consonant A musical sound that is pleasing to the ear. A musical sound that is stable.
Count Off

A group of two or more beats that are counted out loud at the beginning of a piece of music. A Count Off is used to establish the tempo before the music begins.
Dissonant A musical sound that is not pleasing to the ear. A musical sound that is harsh or unstable.
Dotted Half Note A musical note with the rhythmic value of three beats.
Dotted Quarter Note A musical note with the rhythmic value of one and a half beats.
Double Bar Line Two vertical lines placed at the end of a piece of music that signify the end.
Duration

The specific length of a musical note when measured against the beat of the music. The Duration of a note can be equal to the beat, longer than the beat, or shorter than the beat.
Dynamics Pertaining to the loudness or softness of a musical sound. 
Eighth Note A musical note with the rhythmic value of half of a beat. Eighth Notes can be written as individual notes or in groups of two or more notes which are connected by a beam.
Eighth Note Triplet A group of three Eighth Notes with a total rhythmic value of one beat. An Eighth Note Triplet can be used to replace a group of two normal Eighth Notes. An Eighth Note Triplet must be picked slightly faster than normal Eighth Notes in order to fit a group of three notes into the space of a single beat.
Enharmonic Notes Two musical notes that have the same pitch but are written and named using different notation, such as D# and Eb.
Flat An accidental that is found a half step below a letter of the Musical Alphabet. A note that has been lowered by a half step. On the fretboard of the guitar, a Flat note is played a half step or one fret lower than a natural note.
Grace Note A musical note with no rhythmic value. A grace note is notated on the music staff as a very small musical note. This is to show that the grace note is not counted rhythmically and does not have any rhythmic value. A grace note will normally precede a note that DOES have a specific rhythmic value.
Grace Note Slide A guitar technique that is played by picking a single note one string and then sliding the fretted note into a second note on the same string. The second note is sounded by sliding the finger up or down the string without picking the string again. A grace note slide can ascend or descend the string, and can be played with any finger. When playing a grace note slide, the note which is picked has no rhythmic value or count, which means that the sliding motion used by the left hand must be instant, and the movement from the picked note to the slid note must be very fast.
Half Note A musical note with the rhythmic value of two beats.
Half Step The smallest interval used in music, also known as a semitone. The smallest distance between two notes. On the fretboard of the guitar, a Half Step is the distance from one fret to the next.
Harmonic Rhythm The number of beats played on each chord in a chord progression. Also, the number of chords played in each measure of a chord progression. A chord progression with fast Harmonic Rhythm will have two or more chords in each measure of the progression, while a chord progression with slow Harmonic Rhythm will have only one chord in each measure.
Harmony The sound of two or more notes that are played at the same time. When three or more notes are played at the same time, it is called a chord.
Hammer On

One of three different "Legatto" techniques. When playing a hammer on, a single note is picked on one string followed by a second note on the same string that is not picked. The second note is sounded by hammering one of the left hand fingers onto the fretboard without picking the string again. The hammered note is always higher in pitch than the note that is picked. A hammer on can also be called an "Ascending Slur" because the second note always has a higher pitch and is slurred into.
Interval The spacing or distance in pitch between two musical notes. On the fretboard of the guitar, an Interval is the spacing or distance between different note locations.
Intonation When a note is played on the guitar using the bend technique, close attention must be paid to the accuracy of the pitch that is being bent into. The music term intonation is used to describe the accuracy of pitch that must be achieved when bending a note. When a note is bent, the normal pitch of the fretted note is being raised to a higher pitch, and this pitch must be targeted very precisely. Otherwise the bend will sound out of tune. When developing the bending technique, you must build the physical strength to bend the string first, and then develop good intonation so that your bends will target the correct pitch and will sound in tune.
Key When a song is played using the notes of a specific scale, the song is said to be in the Key of that Sale. The music terms Scale and Key are often be used to describe the same thing.
Key Signature One or more accidentals that are written on the music staff at the beginning of a piece of music.  A Key Signature is always written on the music staff directly after the treble clef. The Key Signature tells you what scale will be used within the song.
Legatto Technique The music term legatto is used to describe a series of notes that are played with a smooth or flowing sound. On the guitar, this means that notes will be sounded by the fingers of the left hand without picking the strings. This is done using a combination of three different techniques, which are the "Hammer On", the "Pull Off", and the "Trill" techniques. The combination of these techniques together are used to create a legatto sound. To play legatto, the left hand fingers must be strong enough to sound notes on the fretboard without picking each string individually. Legatto techniques are also referred to as "Slurs" because the notes are not picked individually but are slurred together.
Linear Scale A series of individual notes played in order on a single string of the guitar that follow a specific pattern of intervals. A Linear Scale can be played on any one of the six strings of the guitar.
Major Chord The brightest chord type in music. The most consonant type of chord.
Major Scale One of the most important scale types to learn in music. The Major Scale is built using seven specific notes from the Chromatic Scale. The first letter of the Major Scale is called the Root Note of the scale, which is where the scale gets its name. Once the Root Note is chosen, the remaining notes of the Major Scale are played in order by following a very specific pattern of whole steps and half steps. The eighth letter of the Major Scale is always the Octave of the Root Note. The pattern of Intervals used to build a Major Scale is: Whole Step, Whole Step, Half Step, Whole Step, Whole Step, Whole Step, Half Step. The Major Scale can begin on any letter of the Musical Alphabet as well as any Accidental.
Major Triad A three note chord that is built using the 1st, 3rd and 5th Scale Tones from the Major Scale. A Major Triad can be built from any Major Scale and will always have the same Chord Formula: 1st 3rd 5th. These three notes are the Chord Tones of the Major Triad.
Measure A series of two or more beats that have been grouped together, also called a bar of music.
Melodic Rhythm A series of individual notes or pitches that are played with a specific rhythm.
Melody A series of two or more individual notes or pitches that follow a specific pattern. Some Melodies use only a few notes, while other Melodies use many notes.
Minor Chord When compared to the sound of a Major Chord, the sound of a Minor Chord is dark or sad sounding.
Musical Alphabet The seven letters used to name notes in music, which are A B C D E F G.
Music Staff A group of five horizontal lines that are used to notate the pitch and rhythm of musical notes.
Natural Note A note from the Musical Alphabet which is not sharp or flat. There are seven Natural notes used in music, which are the seven letters of the Musical Alphabet: A B C D E F G
Natural Sign A musical symbol that is used to change a sharp or flat note back to its original pitch.
Octave The interval of 12 half steps that comprise the complete Chromatic Scale. An Octave is a musical note which is 12 half steps above or below another note. Also, the point where the Musical Alphabet begins again. Musical notes that are an Octave apart will have the same letter name but each note will have a different sound or pitch.
Off Beat The notes that are played between each beat in music. Many musical styles use the sound of the Off Beat to create an interesting rhythmic sound. When you tap your foot to the beat of music, your foot is tapping the floor on the beat. When your foot comes back up is where the Off Beat is. Rhythms that emphasize the Off Beat are known as "Synchopated" rhythms.
Open Position Notes that are played within the first four frets of the fretboard of the guitar.
Pick Up A single note or group of notes played at the beginning of a melody before the first full  measure of music. When a song begins with Pick Up notes, the final measure will always be short the exact number of beats used as Pick Ups.
Pitch The frequency of a note determining how high or low it sounds. The difference between the high and low sounds heard in music. A high sound is described as having a high Pitch, while a low sound is described as having a low Pitch.
Position Scale A scale form that covers a range of four or five frets, also known as a "Box Scale" or "Box Pattern". Learning to play scales by position on the guitar is very common. Once the individual positions of a scale are learned, then many scale forms can be combined together to cover the entire range of the instrument using the scale tones of a single Key or scale.
Pull Off One of three different "Legatto" techniques. When playing a pull off, a single note is picked on one string followed by a second note on the same string that is not picked. The second note is sounded by pulling one of the left hand fingers off of the string to sound another note without picking the string again. The second note in a pull off is always a lower pitched note than the note that is picked. A pull off can also be called an "Descending Slur" because the second note always has a lower pitch and is slurred into.
Pre Bend A variation of the bend technique, also known as a "Reverse Bend". When playing a note with the pre bend technique, the fingers of the left hand are used to bend a fretted note on a single string up to a higher pitch before the string is picked. Once the fingers have bent the string into position and the higher pitch has been reached, then the string is picked and the bend is released back to its original pitch.
Quarter Note A musical note with the rhythmic value of one beat.
Relative Pitch The difference in sound between two different pitches. When comparing the sound of two different pitches, one pitch can be described as being higher or lower in relation to the other pitch, which is where the term Relative Pitch comes from.
Rest A moment of silence; a pause or a break in the rhythm of the music. A Rest is played on the guitar by muting the strings using left hand muting or right hand muting.
Reverse Bend A variation of the bend technique, also known as a "Pre Bend". When playing a note with the reverse bend technique, the fingers of the left hand are used to bend a fretted note on a single string up to a higher pitch before the string is picked. Once the fingers have bent the string into position and the higher pitch has been reached, then the string is picked and the bend is released back to its original pitch.
Rhythm The duration of a musical note when measured against the beat of the music. The duration of a rhythm can be equal to the beat, longer than the beat, or shorter than the beat.
Root The Musical Alphabet letter that is used to name a Chord or Scale. The starting point of a Chord or Scale, which is the lowest note or deepest pitch.
Scale

A series of notes from the Musical Alphabet that are played in order following a specific pattern of intervals. The notes of a Scale can be played in succession either ascending or descending.
Scale Tones

The individual notes found within a scale. In music, Scale Tones are numbered beginning with the Root Note of the scale, which is always numbered as "1st". From there, each consecutive Scale Tone is numbered in order until the Octave of the scale is reached, which is always numbered as 8th. The Major Scale is numbered as 1st 2nd 3rd 4th 5th 6th 7th 8th.
Sharp An accidental that is found a half step above a letter of the Musical Alphabet. A note that has been raised by a half step. On the fretboard of the guitar, a Sharp note is played a half step or one fret higher than a natural note.
Style Elements A series of guitar techniques that are used to make the guitar a more expressive instrument. Guitarist use these techniques to give their playing a more emotional quality. Some of the most common and important Style Elements are the hammer on, the pull off, the trill, the valued slide, the grace note slide, vibrato, bending, bend and release, reverse bend, bend and vibrato, as well as many more.
Slash Chord

A Slash Chord is created by replacing the Root Note of a chord with a new note from the Musical Alphabet. The new Root Note will always be the lowest pitch of the chord. The name Slash Chord comes from the way that the chord name is written or notated. To the left of the slash is the original name of the chord, and to the right of the slash is the letter name of the new Root Note, such as A/E or E Minor/G.
Slur A small curved line that connects two different pitches on the music staff. When two notes on the music staff are slurred together, the second note is not picked and is played "Legatto".  When three or more notes on the music staff are slurred together, only the first note is picked and the remaining notes are played "Legatto".
Suspended Chord

A Suspended Chord is created by raising or lowering a note within a Major or Minor chord. Suspended Chords are neither Major or Minor and belong to their own separate category. The two types of Suspended Chords used in music are the Suspended 4 Chord and the Suspended 2 Chord. These new chord types are often abbreviated as Sus Chords, as in Sus 4 or Sus 2. When compared to the sound of a Major or Minor chord, a Suspended Chord will sound tense or unresolved.
Synchopation

A rhythmic pattern that accents or emphasizes the Off Beat of the music. Synchopated rhythms will often include notes that are played on the beat as well as between each beat. The accent or emphasis of the Off Beat can be placed anywhere within the measure.
Tablature A system of music notation designed specifically for the guitar where notes are indicated by the specific fret locations. The Tablature Staff consists of six horizontal lines that are used to represent the six strings of the guitar.
Tempo The speed with which the beat of music is counted. Music that has a slow beat is said to have a slow Tempo, while music that has a fast beat is said to have a fast Tempo. The beat of music is always steady and even, but the Tempo may be slow or fast.
Tie A curved line that connects two or more notes of the same pitch. A Tie is used to combine the rhythmic duration of both notes together. When two notes of the same pitch are Tied together, only the first note be played, and the rhythmic value of the second note will be added to the first note. This means that the second note in a Tie is not played.
Time Signature A pair of numbers written at the beginning of a piece of music that are used to specify the number of beats that are counted within each measure. The top number in a Time Signature tells you how many beats are counted within each measure. The bottom number in a Time Signature tells you what type of note is used to represent each beat within the measure.
Trill

One of three different "Legatto" techniques. A trill is played by combining the hammer on and pull off techniques together. When playing a trill, a single note is picked on one string followed by a second note that is sounded with a hammer on. Then the first note is sounded again by using a pull off. The third note in a trill is the same note that was initially picked, but this note is not picked again. Only the first note in a trill is picked while the other notes are played "Legatto" or "Slurred".
Valued Slide A guitar technique that is played by picking a single note on one string and then sliding the fretted note into a second note on the same string. The second note is sounded by sliding the finger up or down the string without picking the string again. A valued slide can ascend or descend the string, and can be played with any finger. When playing a valued slide, each note will have a specific rhythmic value or count. Close attention must be paid to the correct left hand technique as well as the correct rhythmic count.
Vibrato When playing a note that uses the vibrato technique, a single note is picked on one string and the finger that is used to fret the note is then moved from side to side to "wiggle" or "shake" the string. The left hand finger will continue to fret the note, but the tip of the finger moves the string side to side, which adds a "shaking" sound. Vibrato can be played using any finger of the left hand, and can be done on any fretted note on any string. Guitarists often use many different variations of the vibrato technique.
Whole Note A musical note with the rhythmic value of four beats.
Whole Step The interval of two half steps combined, also known as a whole tone. On the fretboard of the guitar, a Whole Step is the distance of two frets between notes.
Unison Two musical notes that have exactly the same pitch. Two musical notes that are identical in sound.
